One cannot have the concept of a red object without having the concept of an extended object. 
But the word "red" doesn't contain the word "extended." In general, our concepts are interconnected 
in ways in which the corresponding words are not interconnected. This is not an accidental fact about 
the English language or about any other language: it is inherent in what a language is that the 
cognitive abilities corresponding to a person's abilities to use words cannot possibly be reflected in 
semantic relations holding among those words. This fact in its turn is a consequence of the fact that 
expressions are, whereas concepts are not, digital structures, for which reason the ways in which cognitive 
abilities interact cannot possibly bear any significant resemblance to the ways in which expressions interact. 
Consequently, there is no truth to the contention that our thought-processes are identical with, 
or bear any resemblance to, the digital computations that mediate computer-activity.

The relationship between language and thought has long been a subject of philosophical and cognitive inquiry. One intriguing aspect of this relationship is the interconnectedness of concepts in the human mind, which does not always align with the linguistic expressions used to communicate those concepts. For example, the understanding of a red object inevitably involves the concept of an object possessing extension in space. However, the word "red" itself does not linguistically encapsulate the notion of "extended." This discrepancy highlights a fundamental characteristic of languages: they do not necessarily mirror the conceptual connections present in human cognition.

This phenomenon is not a mere linguistic quirk of the English language or any other specific language. Rather, it is intrinsic to the nature of language as a whole. A language serves as a medium for communication, while the cognitive abilities corresponding to a person's linguistic capabilities transcend mere semantic connections among words. The distinction between these cognitive abilities and linguistic expressions can be traced back to the inherent nature of expressions as digital structures, which stand in contrast to the analog and often fluid nature of human thought processes.

The digital nature of linguistic expressions implies that they are discrete and finite, composed of a limited set of symbols and rules for combination. In contrast, concepts in the human mind are more akin to analog structures, capable of nuanced and continuous variation. As a result, there exists a substantial gap between the ways in which cognitive abilities interact and the ways in which linguistic expressions are used. This gap suggests that thought processes cannot be reduced to, nor are they accurately represented by, the digital computations that underpin computer activity.

Empirical studies in cognitive science support this assertion by revealing the complexity of human thought, which often defies simple linguistic description. For instance, research on the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis suggests that while language can influence thought, it does not determine it entirely (Whorf, 1956). Cognitive processes are often more intricate and multifaceted than the language used to express them. Moreover, studies in psycholinguistics demonstrate that the brain processes linguistic and non-linguistic information in fundamentally different ways, further underscoring the distinction between language and thought (Pinker, 1994).

Furthermore, the contention that our thought processes are akin to the digital computations of computers finds little support in current cognitive theories. The computational theory of mind, which posits that mental processes are akin to computer operations, has been challenged by findings that highlight the unique, non-digital characteristics of human cognition (Searle, 1980). For example, the ability of humans to understand context, infer meaning, and engage in creative thought far exceeds the capabilities of digital computation, suggesting that human cognition cannot be fully captured by digital models.

In conclusion, the intricate interplay between language and thought reveals fundamental differences between linguistic expressions and cognitive processes. While language provides a structured means of communication, it does not encapsulate the full complexity of human thought. Empirical evidence and theoretical discourse in the cognitive sciences continue to highlight the limitations of digital computation models in representing the richness of human cognition. Consequently, the assertion that thought processes are equivalent to digital computations does not hold, reinforcing the view that language and thought, while interconnected, are distinct phenomena.

For an explanation to be good isn't for it to be correct. Sometimes the right explanations are bad ones. A story will make this clear. I'm on a bus. The bus driver is smiling. A mystery! Let's talk about language and thought. They're like two dance partners, always moving together but never quite in sync. Picture this: you're trying to describe a red apple. You say "red," but that word doesn’t capture the apple’s roundness or its place in space. You see, the word "red" doesn’t carry the whole picture, just like a single brushstroke doesn't complete a painting. This isn't just a quirk of English; it’s a universal truth about language. Language is a tool, a way to share ideas, but it doesn't always map perfectly onto the way we think.

Imagine language as a set of building blocks. They're finite and fixed, each piece fitting into place according to certain rules. But human thought? It's more like water, fluid and ever-changing, slipping through the gaps left by those rigid blocks. Our thoughts are analog, nuanced, and continuous, while language is digital, discrete, and finite. So, there's this gap—a chasm, really—between the way we think and the way we speak. It’s like trying to capture a river with a net.

Cognitive science backs this up. Take the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis, for instance. It suggests that language shapes thought but doesn’t cage it. Thought is a complex beast, far too wild to be tamed by mere words. Psycholinguistics tells us that the brain handles language differently from other kinds of information, emphasizing that the two are distinct arenas.

Now, some folks might argue that our minds work like computers, running on digital computations. But that’s a stretch. Computers are good at following rules, but human thought? It breaks them. We understand context, infer meanings, and create ideas that no algorithm could predict. Our minds are not just machines ticking away in binary code; they’re something more.

So, what’s the takeaway? Language and thought are intertwined but distinct. Language is a structured means of communication, a way to share the dance of ideas. But it doesn’t capture the full complexity of human thought. The richness of our minds escapes the confines of digital computation models. In the end, thought processes are not just digital computations. They are something else entirely, something uniquely human.
