One cannot grasp the philosophy of transcendental empiricism without understanding its roots in mental content. However, the term "transcendental" does not inherently convey "mental." In general, philosophical doctrines are interconnected in ways that the corresponding words are not. This is not an accidental feature of philosophical language: it is inherent in the nature of philosophy that the cognitive abilities corresponding to one's understanding of concepts cannot be mirrored in the linguistic relations among those concepts. This phenomenon is a consequence of expressions being, unlike concepts, digital structures, thereby precluding any significant resemblance between cognitive interactions and the interactions of expressions. Consequently, the assertion that transcendental empiricism is identical to ordinary empiricism, or bears any resemblance to it, is unfounded.

In this exploration, I critically assess transcendental empiricism, a philosophy that navigates the complex terrain of mental content by reconciling opposing views. John McDowell's minimal empiricism and Richard Gaskin's minimalist empiricism represent distinct strands of this philosophy, which was first articulated by McDowell. This examination unfolds in five segments. Initially, I discuss the Wittgensteinian metaphilosophical stance of transcendental empiricism, setting a foundation for subsequent analysis. Next, I delve into McDowell’s minimal empiricism, providing a comprehensive account. Following this, I critique Gaskin's challenges to McDowell's ideas, arguing that Gaskin's critiques are flawed and that his minimalist empiricism is questionable. Subsequently, I analyze the credentials of McDowell's minimal empiricism, highlighting issues in his linguistic idealism and addressing recent dialogues with Hubert Dreyfus's phenomenology. Dreyfus's charge of the “Myth of the Mental” is affirmed, casting doubt on McDowell's direct realism. I conclude that minimal empiricism fails to resolve the mental content dilemma and note that Tyler Burge effectively challenges disjunctivism, a pivotal component of transcendental empiricism. Nevertheless, I contend that transcendental empiricism remains a compelling alternative in the discourse on mental content.
