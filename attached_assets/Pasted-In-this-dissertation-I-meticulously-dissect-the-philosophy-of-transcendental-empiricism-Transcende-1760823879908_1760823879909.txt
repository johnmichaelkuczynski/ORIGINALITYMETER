In this dissertation, I meticulously dissect the philosophy of transcendental empiricism. Transcendental empiricism, not merely a philosophy of mental content but a complex theoretical construct, seeks to untangle an epistemological quandary of mental content by navigating the chasm between two fundamentally antagonistic content theories. John McDowell's minimal empiricism and Richard Gaskin's minimalist empiricism represent two iterations of transcendental empiricism, with the latter deriving its foundational impulse from McDowell's intellectual ventures. This dissertation is systematically partitioned into five segments. Initially, in the Introduction, I delineate the Wittgensteinian metaphilosophical framework underpinning transcendental empiricism. This metaphilosophical stance forms a foundational plane upon which the ensuing discourse is predicated. Subsequently, I provide a comprehensive exposition of McDowell’s minimal empiricism. Thereafter, I engage with Gaskin's critique and proposed modifications of McDowell's minimal empiricism, positing that (1) Gaskin's critiques lack cogency and (2) Gaskin's minimalist empiricism remains profoundly contentious. In the fourth segment, I interrogate the purported validity of McDowell's minimal empiricism, contending that McDowell's linguistic idealism harbors inherent flaws. I proceed to analyze an emergent dialogue between transcendental empiricism and Hubert Dreyfus’s phenomenology, wherein Dreyfus levels the charge of the “Myth of the Mental.” I uphold the veracity of this accusation, thereby casting McDowell's direct realism into question. Consequently, I assert that minimal empiricism fails to resolve the dilemma of mental content. Lastly, I contend that Tyler Burge effectively destabilizes the doctrine of disjunctivism, a doctrine instrumental to transcendental empiricism. Yet, my ultimate objective is to demonstrate that transcendental empiricism presents a compelling alternative to existing philosophies of mental content.
